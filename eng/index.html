Frequently Asked Questions

Diagnosis of viral infections is currently based on two main methodologies: Reverse Transcription Quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction (RT-qPCR) and serological immunoassay methods that detect viral specific antibodies (IgM and IgG) or antigens. Although RT-qPCR is an extremely sensitive test for SARS-CoV-2 (the virus that causes COVID-19), it has limitations. RT-qPCR requires a high quality nasopharyngeal swab containing sufficient amount of viral RNA. This can be difficult because the amount of viral RNA not only varies tremendously between patients, but also within the same patient depending on the timing of the test and the onset of infection. The swab method is not only very difficult for the patient, but sampling techniques also vary significantly from nurse to nurse. Insufficient quantities of viral RNA samples may give false negative results in the RT-qPCR test. Additionally, the RT-qPCR technique requires highly trained personnel to perform complex RNA extraction steps and the process. Normally, testing a few thousand samples would not be a problem. But the RT-qPCR technique becomes a problem when dealing with a global pandemic if there are millions of people potentially available to test it. This causes delays in tests due to the high demand in healthcare facilities.
